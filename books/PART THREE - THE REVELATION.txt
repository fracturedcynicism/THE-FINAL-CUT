PART THREE: THE REVELATION
CHAPTER 12
The sixth subject was a militant named Khalid who'd been involved with the TTP but was now operating as a middleman between various groups. Reformed militant interested in regional cooperation.
The subject didn't seem particularly reformed. He spoke carefully. When Sarah asked about his transformation, he deflected.
"People change their minds," he said. "They realize they've made mistakes."
---
The plan was to film Khalid, establish final timing data, then allow three days before executing the operation.
But the timing got disrupted.
On the first morning after the Khalid interview, militant forces launched a coordinated assault. The attack came from three vectors simultaneously—north, west, behind the ridge. AK-47s. RPGs. Explosions.
The crew was in the main building.
What happened next was the moment when everything inverted.
---
CHAPTER 13
The attack lasted forty minutes.
What made it notable wasn't the duration but what happened next.
The grip team—who'd spent weeks fumbling with equipment, asking incomprehensible questions, tripping over cables—transformed. Arjun moved to intercept the attacking force. Rohit was suddenly calling in coordinates with the casual efficiency of someone with military authority. Vikram provided suppressive fire with marksmanship that belonged to a trained soldier. Arun moved like someone who'd been trained for exactly this scenario.
James was filming it all. Not because he was stupid, but because as a cinematographer, his first instinct was to document. By the time he understood that the grip team was military, the moment was already recorded.
Sarah and Michael were in the supply room. Derek was maintaining his audio equipment out of pure procedure.
The attack ended as suddenly as it began. The militants withdrew. The compound's defenses held. The crew was alive.
And the grip team's cover was irreversibly blown.
---
CHAPTER 14
When it was over, Arjun came to the main building. He was carrying a weapon, which was the least subtle thing that had happened all day.
"Everyone in the main room," Arjun said. "Now."
The crew assembled. Sarah was trembling. Michael was checking himself for injuries. Derek was counting people. James was still processing what he'd filmed.
"We are not a grip team," Arjun said. "We are Indian Special Operations, Cabinet Secretariat designation. The people you've been interviewing were identified as high-value targets. The interviews provided cover for intelligence gathering and target positioning. Several of your subjects have been eliminated over the past month. Their deaths were attributed to local causes. This attack was organized by hostile elements who became aware of the operation. We defended against that attack. We defended you. And now you know what's actually happening."
Silence.
"The documentary subjects," Sarah said finally. "You killed them."
"We executed operations," Arjun said. "The deaths were our responsibility. The documentary was cover for the operations. You were unwitting participants."
"Why would you tell us?" Michael asked.
"Because you can't unknow it," Arjun said. "Derek has known for weeks. Sarah has known for days. You're all suspicious. We can't operate effectively with a crew that's actively hostile. So we're reframing from unknowing to knowing."
---
The crew left the compound in a military convoy at dawn.
Eight hours to Peshawar. Military escort. Minimal conversation.
In Peshawar, they were deposited at a hotel with a liaison officer named Priya who introduced herself as a producer.
"The project is not over," Priya said. "It's entering its final phase. You're going to edit the footage. You're going to create a coherent narrative. And we're going to ensure that the narrative reaches the appropriate audiences."
"No," Sarah said. "We're going to release an accurate documentary about what we filmed, or we're not releasing anything."
"That's not an option," Priya said. "What you filmed is classified material. It's part of an ongoing operation. You don't have the right to release it without approval."
"I don't care what rights I have," Sarah said. "I'm not going to participate in distortion."
Priya looked at her with something that might have been sympathy. "You've already participated. You filmed it. You documented it. You gave the operation credibility by being an authentic international crew. The distortion is already done. The final edit is just the formalization of something that's already occurred."
