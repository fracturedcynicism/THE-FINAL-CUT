PART TWO: THE PATTERN
CHAPTER 8
The third death came on a Monday.
Jamila Hussain was a women's rights activist. The crew filmed her discussing education initiatives and economic empowerment for women. She talked about how conflict had disrupted traditional gender roles, how some women had found liberation in that disruption.
Jamila died of a sudden illness on Monday evening. Fever that spiked overnight. Complications the local doctor couldn't manage.
Sarah wanted to stop immediately. "Three subjects in two weeks. All dead. We're somehow causing this."
"Three deaths in a conflict zone is statistically unremarkable," Michael said. "People die in conflict zones."
"Not people we just interviewed," Sarah said.
"These deaths are unconnected," Michael continued. "One heart attack, one motorcycle accident, one illness. Different causes. Different locations. The only connection is our documentary. And yes, subjects in our documentary are at higher risk because we're filming in a conflict zone."
He was articulate. This was how operations continued—through reasonable doubt, through appeal to statistical reality.
James was silent. He understood something was wrong but couldn't articulate it.
Derek said nothing. Derek had become complicit through knowledge.
"We continue," Sarah said finally. "But we'll be more careful."
This was the moment of compromise.
---
CHAPTER 9
Over the next two weeks, the crew filmed five more subjects. A textile merchant. A medical worker. A former militant who'd become a teacher. A village elder. A humanitarian aid coordinator.
All of them told stories of resilience. All of them believed they were participating in a documentary about peace-building.
Derek recorded their voices. James filmed their faces. Sarah asked questions that shaped the narrative toward hope. And somewhere in Delhi, people were making decisions about which death would be most effective at which time.
But Sarah wasn't accepting this. Sarah was planning.
---
CHAPTER 10
The fourth subject was a man named Salim Hassan who'd genuinely renounced violence. He talked about the spiritual emptiness of violence, about the way weapons could destroy architecture and bodies but not the structures of fear that violence constructed.
The crew filmed him in a madrassa where he was teaching both Quranic and secular subjects. The madrassa had been established to bridge religious and secular education.
Salim died in his sleep. His wife found him in the morning. No obvious cause. Heart attack, probably.
That night, Sarah came to Thomas's room.
"I know what's happening," she said without preamble. "Derek figured it out weeks ago. I can see it in how he moves. I can see it in how he looks at people we're about to interview."
Thomas closed his laptop.
"There are operations," Sarah said. "People involved in networks that are threats. The documentary provides cover for identifying targets and establishing timing. The deaths are part of the operation."
"Yes," Thomas said.
She was right. There was no point maintaining the fiction.
"And we're just documenting it?" Sarah asked.
"You're documenting authentically," Thomas said. "The crew is authentic. The documentary is real. What's happening alongside it is also real. Both things are occurring simultaneously."
"That's monstrous," Sarah said.
"Yes," Thomas said. "It is."
"Why would you tell me this?"
"Because you're going to figure it out anyway. And because if you figure it out alone, you'll do something desperate. And if you do something desperate, people will get hurt. If I tell you, you might do something smarter."
"What do you want me to do?" Sarah asked.
"Finish the documentary," Thomas said. "Keep filming. Let the operation continue. And understand that once you know this, you're compromised. There's no clean exit anymore."
Sarah looked at Thomas. She understood that he was right. She understood that continuation was safer than resistance.
"I'm going to try something," Sarah said finally. "I'm going to try to preserve the original footage."
"That won't work," Thomas said.
"Maybe not," Sarah said. "But I have to try something that isn't just acceptance."
---
CHAPTER 11
Sarah began documenting in secret.
She started with audio files. Derek was already managing them obsessively. Sarah asked Derek for help creating redundant backups.
"I want to make sure we have backup copies," she said. "In case equipment fails."
Derek understood immediately. Derek agreed.
They began creating secret copies of audio files and storing them on encrypted drives that Sarah kept hidden. They created a system of notation that would allow them to identify which subjects had died and when.
"If this doesn't work," Derek said while they were copying files, "if we can't actually change anything with this material, we at least create a record that the truth existed."
"That's not nothing," Sarah said.
But the audio files alone weren't enough. Sarah needed video. Raw footage. Unedited. Preserved.
James controlled the camera footage. James had no suspicion of what was happening. James still believed the documentary was legitimate.
Sarah tried to copy the raw video files from production drives while James was asleep. The drives were encrypted. The system timed out. By morning, the encrypted drives had been moved by Arjun.
Malik came to Sarah the next afternoon. He didn't say anything directly. He just gave her a look that communicated complete understanding.
That night, Arjun came to Sarah's room.
"You're trying to preserve material," Arjun said. Not a question.
"Yes," Sarah said.
"You understand that makes you a serious problem," Arjun said.
"Yes," Sarah said.
"You understand that trying to expose the operation makes you a target," Arjun said.
"Yes," Sarah said.
"Then why are you doing it?"
Sarah considered lying. She was past the point of successful deception.
"Because I have to try," Sarah said. "Because if I don't try, then I'm just accepting that documentary becomes a weapon and I'm the one holding it. Because some part of me still believes that creating a record of truth matters, even if it doesn't change anything."
Arjun sat down. He looked at Sarah with an expression that combined contempt and something that might have been respect.
"You're going to stop trying to copy the video files," Arjun said. "You're going to stop creating secret backups. You're going to understand that continuing documentary work with this level of attempted sabotage makes you too much of a liability to keep alive during an active operation."
Sarah felt her stomach drop.
"However," Arjun continued, "we're almost done. Another few weeks and the operation concludes. We extract the crew. You go back to your normal life. And you understand, permanently, that you can never speak about this. You can never release the material. You can never tell anyone what happened."
"That's a threat," Sarah said.
"Yes," Arjun said. "It's a very clear threat."
---
Sarah stopped attempting to copy files. She continued filming the remaining interviews. She performed the role of enthusiastic documentarian. She began understanding that resistance within this system didn't work.
Derek noticed that Sarah had stopped. Derek understood why.
James continued filming, unaware of the dynamics around him. James continued believing the documentary was real. James continued creating beautiful cinematography.
And the pattern of deaths continued.
